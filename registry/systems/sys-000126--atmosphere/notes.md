# Atmosphere

## Overview

An atmosphere is the gravitationally retained gaseous envelope of a
planetary body. As a type-level archetype it sits within the geosphere
class alongside hydrosphere, lithosphere, cryosphere, and pedosphere.
The defining structural property is vertical stratification driven by
pressure / temperature / composition gradients in a rotating, gravity-
bound fluid forced primarily by stellar radiation from above and
surface-coupling fluxes from below.

The catalog treats the atmosphere as a *type* (gaseous-envelope
archetype) rather than as Earth's atmosphere specifically. The Earth
case is one canonical instance — distinguished by its biotic
modification (free O2, low-CO2, ozone layer) — but the archetype also
covers Venus's thick CO2 envelope, Mars's thin one, Titan's nitrogen-
methane atmosphere, the gas-giant atmospheres of Jupiter / Saturn /
Uranus / Neptune, and exoplanet atmospheres in general.

## Characteristic Instances

- **Earth's atmosphere.** ~78% N2, ~21% O2, variable H2O, ~420 ppm CO2.
  Four well-defined thermal layers (troposphere, stratosphere,
  mesosphere, thermosphere) with characteristic temperature inversions.
  Hosts the only known biospheric coupling that has produced a free-
  O2 atmosphere, and the only one currently undergoing rapid
  anthropogenic composition change.

- **Venus's atmosphere.** ~92 bar surface pressure, ~96.5% CO2, ~3.5%
  N2, sulfuric-acid clouds. Surface ~735 K; canonical runaway-greenhouse
  end-state and a key control case for understanding habitability
  boundaries.

- **Mars's atmosphere.** ~6 mbar surface pressure, ~95% CO2. Thin and
  cold; supports seasonal CO2 condensation onto polar caps and global
  dust storms. Canonical thin-atmosphere / escape-driven case.

- **Titan's atmosphere.** ~1.5 bar, mostly N2 with ~5% CH4. The only
  other body in the solar system with a thick N2 atmosphere; supports
  a methane hydrological cycle (rain, rivers, lakes) analogous to
  Earth's water cycle.

- **Jupiter's atmosphere.** Predominantly H2/He with no solid lower
  boundary; banded zonal circulation, long-lived storm structures
  (e.g. Great Red Spot). Canonical deep, hydrogen-dominated gas-giant
  case.

## Organizational Neighbors

- **Below (sub-archetypes / components).** Boundary layer, troposphere,
  stratosphere; specific circulation systems (jet streams, monsoons,
  Hadley cells, hurricanes/cyclones); atmospheric chemistry subsystems
  (ozone layer, sulfur cycle in clouds).

- **Same level (peer geospheres).** Hydrosphere, lithosphere,
  cryosphere, biosphere, pedosphere — all coupled through volatile and
  energy exchange. The atmosphere is the most rapidly mixed and most
  globally connected of these.

- **Above (containing systems).** Earth-system / planetary climate
  system (atmosphere coupled with ocean, ice, land surface, biosphere);
  for non-Earth instances, the planetary system as a whole.

## Open Questions

- Where exactly to draw the upper boundary across instances — the
  Karman line is a convention specific to Earth; the underlying
  exobase / homopause / mesopause hierarchy varies with planet.
- How much of the atmosphere's "system identity" is structural (gravity
  + radiation + fluid dynamics) versus compositional (the specific
  volatile inventory). Earth-Venus-Mars share dynamics but differ
  drastically in steady state.
- Treatment of biosphere-atmosphere coupling: at what point does an
  atmosphere become an "evolved" rather than "natural" archetype?
  Earth's free O2 is biotically maintained; this puts Earth's case in
  hybrid territory.

## Known-ill-defined aspects

- Upper-boundary placement (see Open Questions).
- Whether the ionosphere should be treated as a layer of the
  atmosphere or as a coupled magnetospheric subsystem; the two
  perspectives give different boundary descriptions.
- Where to treat aerosol and cloud microphysics — sometimes a
  component of the atmosphere, sometimes treated as its own coupled
  subsystem in climate modeling.
